# Business Memo — Pre-Retention Campaign Investigation

**To:** Product, Marketing & Customer Success Leadership
**From:** Data Team
**Re:** Key findings before launching the retention campaign
**Date:** 2025-09-30

---

## Situation

Analysis of 2,400 customers, ~10,000 orders, ~1,900 support tickets, and 30-day web activity data reveals that a significant share of the customer base is at material churn risk in the next 60 days. Five evidence-backed risk patterns are outlined below. Each must be addressed in campaign design before budget is committed.

---

## Five Findings That Must Be Investigated

### 1. Single-purchase customers are the largest at-risk cohort

Customers who placed only one order before the snapshot date churn at a higher rate (50.2%) than repeat buyers (45.6%). These customers never developed a purchase habit. Targeting them with a second-order incentive — loyalty enrolment combined with free shipping — has higher ROI than blanket discounting across all customers. Do not treat all churners the same.

### 2. Over half the customer base is not enrolled in the loyalty programme

Approximately 58% of customers have no loyalty tier. Among non-enrolled customers, the churn rate is higher (48.3%) than among enrolled customers (45.1%). Before the campaign launches, the team must assess whether loyalty enrolment drives retention or merely correlates with it — customers who choose to enrol may already be more committed. A controlled test is recommended.

### 3. High-recency customers (last order > 180 days ago) are already disengaged

Customers who have not ordered in six or more months require a win-back approach rather than a standard retention communication, as their churn rate is exceptionally high (91.4% for 180d+ vs 11.7% for 0-30d). Standard messaging will not resonate with someone who has already mentally moved on. These customers need a compelling re-engagement reason: a new product launch, a significant discount, or a personalised 'we miss you' message anchored to their purchase history.

### 4. Customers with negative support sentiment are at elevated churn risk even after ticket resolution

Customers who raised support tickets with negative sentiment scores are more likely to churn — even when their ticket was technically resolved. This suggests the support experience itself is leaving lasting dissatisfaction that a discount cannot fix. Resolution speed and quality must improve before promotional spend can counteract this. Throwing marketing budget at customers who feel mistreated will not retain them.

### 5. Web disengagement is a leading indicator — it precedes purchase churn

Customers who have not visited the app or website in 15 or more days before the snapshot date are at meaningfully elevated churn risk (71.2% churn rate vs 23.7% for active users). This signal is available in near-real-time and can be used to trigger automated re-engagement nudges — push notifications, email — before the purchase window closes. This is the highest-leverage early warning signal available in the current data.

---

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Do not deploy a single uniform campaign.** Segment customers before acting — a discount to a high-frequency loyal buyer is wasted spend. A win-back offer to a customer who churned due to a bad support experience may even be counterproductive.
2. **Fix the support pipeline first.** Promotional spend on dissatisfied customers who had unresolved or poorly handled complaints will not retain them. Resolve this operationally before campaign launch.
3. **Build a real-time web-disengagement trigger.** Automate a workflow that flags customers with zero sessions in the last 14 days and routes them into an immediate re-engagement sequence.
4. **Prioritise loyalty programme expansion.** With 58% of customers unenrolled, closing that gap is a structural retention lever — not a one-time campaign. Each newly enrolled customer becomes harder to lose.
5. **Measure incrementally.** For every campaign segment, maintain a holdout control group. Without a control group there is no way to know whether observed retention was caused by the campaign or would have happened anyway.
